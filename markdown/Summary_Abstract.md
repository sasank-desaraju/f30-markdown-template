# Project Summary/Abstract {#project-summaryabstract .unnumbered}

Glioblastoma is the most common malignant primary brain tumor in adults and remains nearly uniformly recurrent despite maximal surgery, radiation, and temozolomide.
CAR T-cell therapy has produced clear clinical responses in selected patients with recurrent glioblastoma, particularly when delivered directly into the central nervous system, but responses are often transient and antigen escape remains a major barrier [@brownRegressionGlioblastomaChimeric2016; @bagleyIntrathecalBivalentCAR2024; @bagleyIntracerebroventricularBivalentCAR2025].
The long-term goal of this project is to develop rational, clinically translatable strategies that make cellular immunotherapy for malignant brain tumors more durable.
The objective of this F30 is to determine whether bivalent targeting of IL13Ra2 and EGFRvIII can delay antigen escape in patient-derived glioblastoma models and to define the phenotype of tumor cells that survive CAR T-cell pressure.

The central hypothesis is that bivalent IL13Ra2/EGFRvIII targeting will improve tumor control relative to monovalent targeting by reducing single-antigen escape, while residual escape will reveal antigen-low and stress-response states that can guide next-generation combination strategies.
This hypothesis will be tested in three independent aims.
Aim 1 will engineer and characterize a bivalent IL13Ra2/EGFRvIII CAR in primary human T cells and benchmark cytotoxicity, cytokine release, and exhaustion-marker expression against monovalent controls.
Aim 2 will define antigen-escape phenotypes after serial in vitro CAR T selective pressure using flow cytometry and bulk RNA sequencing of patient-derived glioblastoma cultures.
Aim 3 will compare bivalent and monovalent CAR T cells in an orthotopic patient-derived xenograft model with locoregional delivery and quantify residual tumor antigen heterogeneity.

This work is significant because it addresses a central failure mode of CAR T-cell therapy for glioblastoma: heterogeneous and dynamically changing antigen expression.
It is innovative because it treats antigen escape as a measurable evolutionary phenotype rather than only a post-treatment observation.
The proposed training plan will prepare the candidate for a career as a neurosurgeon-scientist by integrating cellular immunotherapy, patient-derived tumor modeling, quantitative image analysis, and rigorous translational study design.
