# Candidate Section {#candidate-section .unnumbered}

## 2. Goals, Preparedness and Potential {#goals-preparedness-and-potential .unnumbered}

### A. Overall Training Goals {#a.-overall-training-goals .unnumbered}

My long-term career goal is to become a neurosurgeon-scientist who develops cellular immunotherapies for malignant brain tumors.
This career requires fluency in neurosurgical oncology, tumor immunology, cellular engineering, quantitative pathology, and early translational study design.
The F30 period is the ideal time to build this foundation because it allows my dissertation research, clinical identity, and mentorship structure to be aligned around a coherent translational problem.

My immediate training goal is to learn how to design rigorous preclinical studies that can inform future cellular-therapy trials.
The proposed project will train me to engineer and benchmark CAR T-cell reagents, use patient-derived glioblastoma models, quantify antigen escape, and interpret residual-disease biology.
I will also learn how to communicate preclinical findings in a way that is useful to clinicians, immunologists, and trialists.

### B. Candidate Preparedness {#b.-candidate-preparedness .unnumbered}

I entered medical school interested in the interface between surgery, cancer biology, and engineering.
During my preclinical training, I developed a strong foundation in molecular biology, immunology, and quantitative analysis.
My PhD work has strengthened my ability to design experiments, analyze complex datasets, and work across disciplinary boundaries.

My prior research experiences prepared me to ask mechanistic questions but also showed me the limits of single-method training.
For cellular therapy to become meaningful for GBM patients, technical innovation must be paired with knowledge of neurosurgical tissue access, tumor heterogeneity, immune resistance, and clinical feasibility.
This F30 will give me the integrated training needed to bridge those domains.

### C. Candidate's Self-Assessment {#c.-candidates-self-assessment .unnumbered}

My strengths are quantitative thinking, persistence with technically difficult projects, and the ability to translate between clinicians and bench scientists.
I am comfortable learning new analytic methods and building reproducible workflows.
I also have a clear clinical motivation because malignant brain tumors are among the diseases that drew me toward neurosurgery.

My training gaps are equally clear.
I need deeper expertise in CAR design, primary T-cell handling, orthotopic brain-tumor models, and the regulatory path from preclinical evidence to early-phase trials.
I also need formal mentorship in how neurosurgeon-scientists protect research time while maintaining clinical excellence.
The proposed sponsor team directly addresses these gaps.

### D. Scientific Perspective {#d.-scientific-perspective .unnumbered}

GBM is not a single uniform target.
It is a spatially heterogeneous and adaptive disease.
For this reason, I believe the next generation of cellular immunotherapy must be designed around resistance from the beginning.
The proposed project reflects that perspective by focusing on antigen escape as the central biological problem rather than treating it as an afterthought.

This F30 will help me develop a research identity grounded in rigorous translational modeling.
By the end of the award period, I expect to have the technical foundation, mentorship relationships, and preliminary data needed to pursue residency training with a durable research program in neurosurgical oncology.
