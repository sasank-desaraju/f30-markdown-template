## 4. Research Training Project Specific Aims {#research-training-project-specific-aims .unnumbered}

Glioblastoma (GBM) is the most common malignant primary brain tumor in adults and remains fatal for most patients despite maximal multimodality therapy.
Adoptive cellular immunotherapy offers a compelling strategy because CAR T cells can be engineered to recognize tumor-associated antigens and can be delivered locoregionally to bypass systemic trafficking barriers.
Early clinical studies targeting IL13Ra2, EGFR, and related glioma antigens have shown that intracranial CAR T-cell delivery can produce radiographic tumor regression, but responses are often incomplete or non-durable [@brownRegressionGlioblastomaChimeric2016; @bagleyIntrathecalBivalentCAR2024; @chanChimericAntigenReceptor2025; @bagleyIntracerebroventricularBivalentCAR2025].
A major barrier is antigen escape: tumor populations with heterogeneous antigen expression can survive single-antigen pressure and repopulate the lesion.

The long-term goal of my research program is to develop cellular immunotherapies that are biologically robust enough for the heterogeneity of malignant brain tumors.
The objective of this F30 is to test whether bivalent IL13Ra2/EGFRvIII targeting delays antigen escape in patient-derived GBM models and to define the phenotypes that remain after CAR T-cell selection.
My central hypothesis is that bivalent targeting will improve tumor control relative to monovalent CAR T cells by reducing single-antigen escape, while residual antigen-low populations will reveal convergent transcriptional programs that can guide future combination strategies.
This hypothesis is supported by clinical evidence that dual-antigen CAR T cells can be delivered to the CNS and induce tumor regression, and by preclinical evidence that IL13Ra2 and EGFRvIII mark clinically relevant but heterogeneous GBM populations [@bagleyIntrathecalBivalentCAR2024; @brownGliomaIL13Ra2Associated2013; @brownRegressionGlioblastomaChimeric2016].

**Aim 1: Engineer and characterize a bivalent IL13Ra2/EGFRvIII CAR in primary human T cells.**
I will construct a bivalent CAR and compare it with monovalent IL13Ra2 and EGFRvIII CARs in primary human T cells.
Using a panel of patient-derived GBM cultures with independently measured antigen profiles, I will quantify antigen-specific cytotoxicity, cytokine release, T-cell expansion, and exhaustion-marker expression.
Expected outcome: a benchmarked bivalent reagent and a rigorous comparison of potency and activation phenotype across antigen contexts.
Independence: the downstream aims do not require this internal construct to outperform published comparators.

**Aim 2: Define the antigen-escape phenotype under CAR T selective pressure in vitro.**
I will expose patient-derived GBM cultures to serial CAR T-cell pressure and recover surviving tumor populations.
Flow cytometry will quantify IL13Ra2 and EGFRvIII expression before and after selection, while bulk RNA sequencing will identify transcriptional programs enriched in antigen-low survivors.
Expected outcome: a reproducible map of antigen and transcriptional changes associated with escape from monovalent and bivalent pressure.
Independence: this aim can use a clinically relevant benchmark CAR if the Aim 1 reagent requires further optimization.

**Aim 3: Test whether bivalent targeting improves tumor control in an orthotopic patient-derived model.**
I will compare bivalent and monovalent CAR T cells after locoregional delivery in an orthotopic patient-derived xenograft model.
The primary endpoint will be survival, with secondary endpoints including bioluminescent tumor burden and multiplex immunofluorescence quantification of residual tumor antigen heterogeneity.
Expected outcome: preclinical evidence defining whether bivalent targeting improves tumor control and how residual disease adapts.
Independence: if the internally engineered bivalent CAR is not suitable for in vivo testing, Aim 3 will use a published bivalent design as the test article.

Successful completion of these aims will establish an integrated experimental and analytic framework for studying antigen escape in GBM cellular immunotherapy.
The training environment and mentorship team will prepare me to lead translational neurosurgical oncology research that moves between operating room specimens, mechanistic modeling, and early-phase therapeutic development.
