### Draft Sponsor Letter: Charles Hermite {.unnumbered}

Dear Review Committee,

I am pleased to provide my strongest support for Allie Gator's NIH F30 application, "Overcoming Antigen Escape with a Bivalent CAR Against Patient-Derived Glioblastoma."
Allie is an exceptional synthetic MD/PhD student whose career goal is to become a neurosurgeon-scientist developing cellular immunotherapies for malignant brain tumors.
Her proposed project is well matched to that goal because it connects a clinically urgent problem, recurrent glioblastoma, to a tractable and rigorous preclinical question: how to reduce and measure antigen escape after CAR T-cell pressure.

My laboratory will serve as the primary home for Allie's dissertation research.
We will provide access to patient-derived glioblastoma cultures, standard operating procedures for tissue processing and culture maintenance, co-culture assay infrastructure, and mentorship in experimental design and data interpretation.
Allie will meet with me weekly during active experimental phases and at least twice monthly during analysis and manuscript periods.
At each meeting, we will review primary data, troubleshoot technical issues, and connect experimental decisions to the overall dissertation and training plan.

The project is feasible because the laboratory already maintains the core model systems and workflows needed for the proposed studies.
The most important scientific risk is that the internally engineered bivalent CAR may not outperform monovalent comparators.
This risk is appropriate for an F30 because the application includes defined alternatives that preserve the training value and scientific question.
If the internal construct underperforms, Allie will still complete rigorous escape phenotyping with benchmark reagents and can use a published bivalent comparator for in vivo testing.

I am committed to Allie's development as an independent physician-scientist.
In addition to technical mentorship, I will provide guidance on manuscript preparation, conference presentations, grant-writing strategy, residency planning, and long-term research identity.
I will also coordinate the mentoring committee so that Allie receives integrated feedback from neuro-oncology, immunoengineering, and quantitative pathology perspectives.
I believe this F30 will provide the protected time and structured training needed for Allie to become a leader in translational neurosurgical oncology.

Sincerely,

Charles Hermite, MD, PhD
